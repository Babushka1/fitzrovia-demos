Amenity Premium · Hedonic Regression

← All demos
The question this answers: how many dollars of monthly rent does each amenity actually command? Hedonic regression — ordinary least squares — answers this by fitting a rent equation to the entire portfolio at once. Each coefficient is the attributed premium: holding unit size, floor, building age, and subway distance constant, how much more do units in buildings with a pool rent for? The same method is used in appraisal and urban economics. With Fitzrovia's rent roll as the input, the recovered coefficients become the attributed premiums in the Amenity ROI dashboard.

The Model Equation

One equation, solved in closed form
rent = β₀ + β₁·sqft + β₂·bedrooms + β₃·floor
     + β₄·buildingAge + β₅·subwayDist
     + β₆·pool + β₇·coworking + β₈·gym
     + β₉·petSpa + β₁₀·concierge + ε

β̂ = (XᵀX)⁻¹ Xᵀy    // ordinary least squares

// β₆…β₁₀ ARE the amenity premiums:
// "holding everything else constant, units in
//  buildings with a pool rent for β₆ more."
Why the ± matters: amenity coefficients come with error bars that widen as the number of buildings shrinks. Any estimate without an error bar is false precision. Within-portfolio comparisons — two similar buildings, one amenity different — tighten the estimate considerably. Small print: hedonics measures association; bundled amenities and overall building quality partially confound each other.

What the coefficients become

From regression output to capex decision

Each amenity coefficient is a revenue estimate. Pair it with cost-per-use data from the Amenity ROI dashboard and you have both sides of any capex decision: β₆ (pool premium) × occupancy × average lease term is the revenue side; maintenance, staffing, and capex amortisation is the cost side. The model makes the revenue side rigorous rather than assumed.

Data requirements: the model needs variation across buildings. If every Fitzrovia building has a gym, the gym coefficient can't be identified from the rent data alone — there's nothing to compare it against. The estimate improves with market comps from comparable buildings that lack the amenity, or with before-and-after data from an amenity addition or removal.
