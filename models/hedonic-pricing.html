Amenity Premium · Hedonic Regression

← All demos
The question this answers: "how many dollars of monthly rent does each amenity actually command?" We generate 3,000 synthetic units whose rents are built from hidden ground-truth prices (so much per sq ft, so much per floor, $65 if the building has a pool…) plus market noise. Then ordinary least squares — the same regression used in appraisal and urban economics — has to recover those prices from rents alone. With real data, the inputs are your rent roll and market comps, and the recovered coefficients are the attributed premiums in the Amenity ROI dashboard.

Why the ± matters: with only ~10 buildings, amenity coefficients come with wide error bars — this page simulates 12 buildings and you can see the uncertainty directly. Any consultant who quotes an amenity premium without an error bar is selling false precision. Small print: hedonics measures association; bundled amenities and building quality partially confound each other. Within-portfolio comparisons (two similar buildings, one amenity different) tighten it.

The Entire Model

One equation, solved in closed form (normal equations)
rent = β₀ + β₁·sqft + β₂·bedrooms + β₃·floor
     + β₄·buildingAge + β₅·subwayDist
     + β₆·pool + β₇·coworking + β₈·gym+
     + β₉·petSpa + β₁₀·concierge + ε

β̂ = (XᵀX)⁻¹ Xᵀy    // ordinary least squares

// β₆…β₁₀ ARE the amenity premiums:
// "holding everything else constant, units in
//  buildings with a pool rent for β₆ more."
